LIVER FUNCTION TESTS
ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase)
ALT is an enzyme found predominantly in liver cells, making it one of the most specific markers of liver injury. When hepatocytes are damaged or destroyed, ALT leaks into the bloodstream, causing serum levels to rise. It plays a role in amino acid metabolism, catalyzing the transfer of an amino group from alanine to alpha-ketoglutarate.
Normal ranges vary slightly by laboratory, but generally accepted values are 7–56 U/L for adults. Men tend to run slightly higher than women, with male reference ranges often up to 40–56 U/L and female ranges up to 35–45 U/L. In children, values can be higher during periods of rapid growth.
Elevated ALT is most commonly caused by non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), viral hepatitis (A, B, C), alcoholic liver disease, drug-induced liver injury (particularly paracetamol, statins, antituberculosis drugs), and autoimmune hepatitis. Mild elevations (1–3× upper limit of normal) are often due to fatty liver or medication effects. Levels exceeding 10× normal suggest acute hepatitis or ischemic hepatitis. Very high elevations (>1000 U/L) are characteristic of acute viral hepatitis, ischemic hepatitis, or acetaminophen toxicity.
Low ALT has limited clinical significance on its own. However, in advanced cirrhosis, ALT may paradoxically fall as the liver loses functional mass and has fewer hepatocytes to release the enzyme. A falling ALT in the context of worsening clinical status should not be interpreted as improvement.
ALT levels above 1000 U/L are clinically urgent and warrant immediate investigation and hospital admission. Values rising sharply alongside INR elevation and encephalopathy signal acute liver failure, which is a medical emergency.

AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase)
AST is an enzyme involved in amino acid metabolism, found in the liver, heart, skeletal muscle, kidneys, and red blood cells. Because it is less liver-specific than ALT, elevated AST can reflect pathology in multiple organ systems. The AST:ALT ratio is diagnostically useful — a ratio greater than 2:1 suggests alcoholic liver disease, while a ratio less than 1 suggests viral hepatitis or NAFLD.
Normal values are generally 10–40 U/L in adults. Men typically have slightly higher reference ranges than women. Values may be mildly elevated in athletes due to skeletal muscle turnover.
Elevated AST occurs in liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis, liver ischemia), acute myocardial infarction, skeletal muscle disease or trauma, hemolysis, pancreatitis, and pulmonary embolism. In alcoholic hepatitis, AST is typically elevated with a disproportionately lower ALT, and rarely exceeds 300 U/L despite significant liver damage. In acute viral hepatitis, both AST and ALT may rise into the thousands.
Low AST is not typically clinically significant. As with ALT, very low values in advanced liver disease may reflect depletion of hepatocyte mass rather than good liver health.
A rapidly rising AST alongside ALT, particularly above 1000 U/L, is urgent and suggests acute hepatocellular injury requiring immediate assessment. When elevated together with troponin and CK, the source may be cardiac or muscular rather than hepatic.

ALP (Alkaline Phosphatase)
ALP is an enzyme produced in several tissues including the liver (bile duct epithelium), bone (osteoblasts), intestine, kidneys, and placenta. In adults, the main sources are liver and bone. ALP is particularly sensitive to biliary obstruction and infiltrative liver disease, making it a key marker of cholestatic injury.
Normal adult values are approximately 44–147 U/L. Children and adolescents have significantly higher levels, sometimes two to three times the adult range, due to active bone growth — this is entirely physiological. Pregnant women in the third trimester also have elevated ALP from placental production. Post-menopausal women may have mildly higher levels than pre-menopausal women.
Elevated ALP in the context of liver disease suggests cholestasis — either intrahepatic (primary biliary cholangitis, drug-induced cholestasis, infiltrative diseases like sarcoidosis, lymphoma, or metastatic cancer) or extrahepatic (bile duct obstruction by gallstones, strictures, or pancreatic cancer). Isolated ALP elevation with normal ALT and GGT more likely reflects a bone source — Paget's disease, bone metastases, healing fractures, or hyperparathyroidism. Confirming the source can be done by measuring GGT simultaneously; if GGT is also elevated, the source is hepatobiliary.
Low ALP is uncommon but can occur in hypothyroidism, pernicious anemia, zinc deficiency, and a rare genetic condition called hypophosphatasia, in which deficient ALP activity leads to impaired bone mineralization.
ALP levels more than 3–4× the upper limit of normal in association with jaundice and right upper quadrant pain are clinically urgent, suggesting biliary obstruction requiring urgent imaging and possible intervention.

GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase)
GGT is an enzyme found on the surface of cells in many organs, with the highest concentrations in the liver, kidneys, pancreas, and intestine. It is highly sensitive to alcohol consumption and is often used to confirm a hepatic source when ALP is elevated. GGT is also induced by many medications and is sensitive to even moderate, regular alcohol intake.
Normal values are approximately 9–48 U/L in adults. Men consistently have higher reference ranges than women (up to 65 U/L in some laboratories). GGT rises with age in both sexes.
Elevated GGT is seen in chronic alcohol use (even without significant liver damage), non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, cholestatic liver disease, hepatitis, pancreatitis, and with medications such as phenytoin, carbamazepine, rifampicin, and barbiturates. Because it is so sensitive to alcohol, it is commonly used to monitor abstinence. In biliary obstruction, GGT tends to parallel ALP. In isolation, an elevated GGT with normal other liver tests most commonly reflects alcohol use, enzyme-inducing drugs, or NAFLD.
Low GGT has no established clinical significance. It is not typically interpreted in isolation at low levels.
An isolated extremely high GGT (e.g., >10× normal) with other markers of liver dysfunction, jaundice, and coagulopathy warrants urgent assessment. In context, it contributes to the clinical picture of acute or severe liver disease rather than being independently urgent.

Bilirubin (Total, Direct, Indirect)
Bilirubin is the breakdown product of heme from red blood cell destruction. Unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin is transported to the liver bound to albumin, conjugated in hepatocytes to a water-soluble form (direct bilirubin), and excreted into bile. Measuring both fractions helps localize the cause of jaundice — pre-hepatic, hepatic, or post-hepatic.
Normal total bilirubin is 3–17 µmol/L (0.2–1.0 mg/dL) in adults. Direct (conjugated) bilirubin is normally less than 5 µmol/L. Neonates have physiologically elevated bilirubin in the first week of life, with values up to 200 µmol/L considered within manageable range depending on gestational age and feeding status. Jaundice becomes visible clinically at levels above approximately 35 µmol/L (2 mg/dL).
Elevated indirect bilirubin points to increased hemolysis (hemolytic anemia, sickle cell disease, transfusion reactions), ineffective erythropoiesis, or impaired conjugation (Gilbert's syndrome, Crigler-Najjar syndrome, neonatal jaundice). Elevated direct bilirubin suggests hepatocellular disease (viral hepatitis, alcoholic hepatitis, cirrhosis) or biliary obstruction (gallstones, cholangiocarcinoma, pancreatic cancer, primary sclerosing cholangitis). Both fractions rise together in combined pathology.
Low bilirubin is not clinically significant and requires no action.
In neonates, bilirubin above 340–425 µmol/L (depending on gestational age and postnatal hours) is a medical emergency due to the risk of kernicterus. In adults, rapidly rising bilirubin above 200–250 µmol/L with coagulopathy and encephalopathy indicates acute liver failure — a life-threatening emergency requiring specialist care.

Albumin
Albumin is the most abundant plasma protein, synthesized exclusively by the liver. It maintains oncotic pressure, transports substances including hormones, drugs, fatty acids, and bilirubin, and serves as a buffer in acid-base balance. Its long half-life of about 20 days means it reflects sustained liver synthetic function rather than acute changes, making it a valuable marker of chronic liver disease severity.
Normal serum albumin is 35–50 g/L in adults. Levels tend to decline slightly with advanced age. Albumin is lower in pregnancy due to haemodilution. It is one component of the Child-Pugh score for liver cirrhosis severity.
Low albumin (hypoalbuminaemia) is common and has multiple causes: chronic liver disease (cirrhosis, chronic hepatitis), protein-losing conditions (nephrotic syndrome, protein-losing enteropathy), malnutrition or malabsorption, chronic inflammatory states (albumin is a negative acute phase reactant, falling during systemic inflammation), and prolonged critical illness. Low albumin affects drug binding and increases the free fraction of highly protein-bound drugs, altering their pharmacokinetics significantly.
Elevated albumin (hyperalbuminaemia) is rare and almost always indicates dehydration or haemoconcentration rather than true overproduction. It has no specific diagnostic significance beyond volume status.
Albumin below 25 g/L is clinically significant and associated with increased risk of complications including peripheral oedema, ascites, impaired wound healing, and altered drug metabolism. In critically ill patients, it is a strong predictor of mortality. Acute falls in albumin combined with rising bilirubin and INR indicate decompensating liver function and require urgent specialist review.

KIDNEY FUNCTION TESTS
Creatinine
Creatinine is a metabolic waste product generated at a relatively constant rate from the breakdown of creatine phosphate in muscle. It is freely filtered by the glomerulus and not significantly reabsorbed, making it a reliable endogenous marker of glomerular filtration rate. Serum creatinine rises as filtration falls, but because the relationship is non-linear, significant renal function must be lost before creatinine rises detectably above normal.
Normal serum creatinine is approximately 60–110 µmol/L (0.7–1.2 mg/dL) in adult men and 45–90 µmol/L (0.5–1.0 mg/dL) in adult women. Muscle mass strongly influences baseline creatinine — athletes and muscular individuals have higher values, while elderly patients, women, and those with low muscle mass have lower values. Children have lower creatinine levels, rising progressively through adolescence.
Elevated creatinine indicates reduced glomerular filtration, which may be pre-renal (dehydration, heart failure, blood loss reducing renal perfusion), intrinsic renal (acute tubular necrosis, glomerulonephritis, interstitial nephritis, chronic kidney disease), or post-renal (urinary tract obstruction). Non-renal causes of elevated creatinine include high dietary meat intake, rhabdomyolysis (massive creatinine release from muscle breakdown), and certain drugs such as trimethoprim and cimetidine, which block tubular secretion without affecting true GFR.
Low creatinine reflects reduced muscle mass, as seen in cachexia, malnutrition, advanced age, prolonged illness, and muscle wasting diseases. It is not a marker of renal pathology but may mask underlying renal impairment if the clinician uses creatinine alone without calculating eGFR.
Creatinine above 500 µmol/L, or any acute doubling of baseline, warrants urgent renal review. Creatinine rising alongside hyperkalaemia, metabolic acidosis, and oliguria constitutes acute kidney injury requiring immediate management.

BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)
Urea is the primary end product of protein catabolism in the liver, formed from the breakdown of amino acids in the urea cycle. It is freely filtered by the glomerulus and partially reabsorbed, particularly in states of low urine flow. BUN reflects both renal excretion and protein catabolism, making it less specific for renal function than creatinine. The BUN-to-creatinine ratio (normal 10:1 to 20:1) is useful for differentiating causes of renal impairment.
Normal BUN is approximately 7–20 mg/dL (2.5–7.1 mmol/L as urea) in adults. Values tend to be slightly higher in elderly individuals and men. Normal dietary protein intake significantly influences BUN, with high-protein diets raising levels without any renal impairment.
Elevated BUN suggests decreased renal perfusion (pre-renal azotaemia from dehydration, heart failure, shock), intrinsic renal disease, or post-renal obstruction. A BUN-to-creatinine ratio above 20:1 strongly suggests a pre-renal cause or upper gastrointestinal bleeding (where blood is digested and absorbed as protein). High BUN is also seen in high protein intake, increased catabolism (fever, trauma, corticosteroid use, burns), and in patients on parenteral nutrition.
Low BUN reflects reduced urea production, most commonly from severe liver disease (impaired urea cycle), low protein intake, malnutrition, or overhydration. Very low BUN in a patient with elevated creatinine may suggest advanced liver disease complicating renal assessment.
BUN alone is rarely the basis for urgent intervention, but in the context of symptomatic uraemia (confusion, pericarditis, nausea, asterixis), BUN levels above 100 mg/dL (36 mmol/L) may indicate a need for urgent dialysis consideration alongside other clinical and biochemical parameters.

eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)
eGFR is a calculated estimate of the kidney's filtering capacity, derived from serum creatinine using equations that account for age, sex, and in some formulas, race. The most widely used current equation is the CKD-EPI equation. It provides a more clinically meaningful assessment of renal function than creatinine alone and is used to stage chronic kidney disease.
Normal eGFR is greater than 90 mL/min/1.73m² in healthy adults. eGFR naturally declines with age, averaging a loss of approximately 1 mL/min/1.73m² per year after the age of 40. An eGFR of 60–89 with no other markers of kidney damage may be normal for age in older adults. CKD is staged from G1 (eGFR ≥90) through G5 (eGFR <15), with G3 divided into G3a (45–59) and G3b (30–44).
A declining eGFR indicates progressive loss of nephron function, which may result from diabetic nephropathy, hypertensive nephrosclerosis, glomerulonephritis, polycystic kidney disease, recurrent infections, obstructive uropathy, or drug nephrotoxicity. The rate of decline is important — rapid falls suggest acute kidney injury superimposed on chronic disease or a new active process. eGFR below 60 triggers referral guidelines and medication dose adjustments.
An eGFR that is unexpectedly high may reflect a hyperfiltration state, as seen in early diabetic nephropathy, pregnancy, or high cardiac output states. This does not reflect better renal health and can precede progressive decline.
eGFR below 15 mL/min/1.73m² defines kidney failure (CKD G5) and requires urgent nephrology review for planning of renal replacement therapy. An acute fall in eGFR by 25% or more from baseline, or an absolute drop placing the patient below 30 within hours to days, constitutes acute kidney injury requiring immediate assessment and management.

Uric Acid
Uric acid is the final oxidation product of purine metabolism in humans, produced mainly in the liver and excreted primarily by the kidneys (two-thirds) and gut (one-third). Unlike most mammals, humans lack the enzyme uricase, making us more susceptible to hyperuricaemia. Uric acid levels reflect the balance between purine intake, endogenous production, and renal excretion.
Normal serum uric acid is approximately 200–420 µmol/L (3.4–7.0 mg/dL) in adult men and 140–360 µmol/L (2.4–6.0 mg/dL) in pre-menopausal women. Post-menopausal women approach male values due to declining oestrogen, which normally promotes uric acid excretion. Values vary by ethnicity and dietary habits.
Elevated uric acid (hyperuricaemia) predisposes to gout — deposition of monosodium urate crystals in joints, soft tissues, and kidneys. Causes include high purine diet (organ meats, shellfish, red meat, beer), decreased renal excretion (chronic kidney disease, thiazide diuretics, cyclosporine, low-dose aspirin, lead nephropathy), and increased production (myeloproliferative disorders, haemolysis, psoriasis, tumour lysis syndrome). Asymptomatic hyperuricaemia is common and does not itself require treatment. Hyperuricaemia is also associated with hypertension, metabolic syndrome, and cardiovascular disease.
Low uric acid (hypouricaemia) occurs with xanthine oxidase inhibitor therapy (allopurinol, febuxostat), hereditary xanthinuria, SIADH (which promotes uric acid excretion), Fanconi syndrome, and Wilson's disease. It is generally benign but rare hereditary forms can cause urolithiasis with xanthine stones.
Uric acid above 700 µmol/L (12 mg/dL) in the context of tumour lysis syndrome is a medical emergency, associated with acute nephropathy, dangerous hyperkalaemia, hyperphosphataemia, and hypocalcaemia requiring urgent rasburicase, aggressive hydration, and electrolyte management.

THYROID PANEL
TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)
TSH is secreted by the anterior pituitary in response to thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH) from the hypothalamus. It stimulates the thyroid gland to produce T3 and T4. Because TSH responds to even small changes in circulating thyroid hormone levels through a logarithmic negative feedback loop, it is the most sensitive first-line test for detecting thyroid dysfunction. A normal TSH effectively excludes significant primary thyroid disease in most outpatient settings.
Normal TSH in adults is approximately 0.4–4.0 mIU/L, though some guidelines use 0.5–5.0 mIU/L. TSH reference ranges shift with age — elderly individuals commonly have TSH in the range of 1.0–7.0 mIU/L as a normal variant. In the first trimester of pregnancy, TSH is physiologically suppressed due to hCG stimulation of TSH receptors, with normal values of 0.1–2.5 mIU/L. Neonates have a transient TSH surge after birth, with levels up to 40 mIU/L in the first day.
Elevated TSH indicates primary hypothyroidism (where the thyroid gland fails and the pituitary compensates by producing more TSH), or rarely TSH-secreting pituitary adenoma. Common causes of primary hypothyroidism include Hashimoto's thyroiditis, radioiodine treatment, thyroidectomy, iodine deficiency, and lithium or amiodarone therapy. Subclinical hypothyroidism is defined as elevated TSH with normal free T4.
Low TSH indicates hyperthyroidism (the pituitary is suppressed by excess thyroid hormone), or secondary/central hypothyroidism (where pituitary failure reduces TSH production despite low thyroid hormone). Common causes of a suppressed TSH include Graves' disease, toxic multinodular goitre, toxic adenoma, and over-replacement with levothyroxine.
A TSH below 0.1 mIU/L in a symptomatic patient, or below 0.01 mIU/L (severely suppressed), warrants urgent thyroid function evaluation for thyroid storm risk. TSH above 10 mIU/L with symptoms of severe hypothyroidism (pericardial effusion, reduced consciousness) suggests myxoedema and requires urgent treatment.

T3 (Triiodothyronine)
T3 is the biologically active thyroid hormone, approximately three to four times more potent than T4. About 20% of circulating T3 is secreted directly by the thyroid gland, with the majority converted peripherally from T4 by deiodinase enzymes in the liver, kidneys, and other tissues. T3 acts on nuclear receptors to regulate metabolism, cardiac function, growth, and development. Measuring total T3 includes both bound and free fractions.
Normal total T3 in adults is approximately 1.1–2.6 nmol/L (70–170 ng/dL). Free T3 (unbound, biologically active) is approximately 3.5–7.8 pmol/L. T3 levels are lower in neonates and rise through childhood, stabilising in adulthood. T3 falls modestly in elderly individuals.
Elevated T3 occurs in hyperthyroidism — particularly early or mild Graves' disease, where T3 may rise disproportionately before T4 becomes overtly elevated. T3 toxicosis is a distinct entity where only T3 is elevated. Other causes include toxic adenoma, toxic multinodular goitre, and excessive T3 supplement ingestion. T3 also rises after iodine-containing contrast media in susceptible individuals.
Low T3 occurs in hypothyroidism but also importantly in non-thyroidal illness syndrome (formerly euthyroid sick syndrome), where peripheral conversion of T4 to T3 is reduced during serious systemic illness, surgery, starvation, or severe metabolic stress. In this setting, TSH and T4 may be normal or low-normal, and low T3 does not represent true hypothyroidism. Treating low T3 in critically ill patients with thyroid hormone has not been shown to improve outcomes.
Dramatically elevated free T3 in a symptomatic patient (tachyarrhythmia, fever, altered consciousness) raises concern for thyroid storm, which is a medical emergency requiring immediate beta-blockade, antithyroid drugs, iodine, corticosteroids, and supportive care.

T4 (Total Thyroxine)
T4 is the primary secretory product of the thyroid gland, produced from the iodination of tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin. It circulates largely bound to thyroxine-binding globulin (TBG), transthyretin, and albumin, with less than 0.05% as free, biologically active hormone. T4 serves as a prohormone, converted peripherally to the active T3. Measuring total T4 includes both bound and free fractions and can be affected by changes in binding proteins.
Normal total T4 in adults is approximately 60–150 nmol/L (4.6–11.2 µg/dL). Levels are higher in pregnancy due to oestrogen-driven increases in TBG. Newborns have elevated T4 which normalises over the first weeks of life. Oral contraceptives and oestrogen therapy also raise total T4 by increasing TBG, without indicating true hyperthyroidism.
Elevated total T4 occurs in hyperthyroidism but also with elevated binding proteins (pregnancy, oestrogen therapy, familial dysalbuminaemia) without any change in free hormone. In true hyperthyroidism (Graves' disease, toxic goitre), free T4 is elevated and TSH is suppressed. Amiodarone inhibits T4 to T3 conversion, causing elevated total and free T4 with a paradoxically normal or elevated TSH in the euthyroid amiodarone-treated patient.
Low total T4 occurs in primary or central hypothyroidism, low TBG states (nephrotic syndrome, chronic liver disease, androgen therapy, severe illness), and with drugs including phenytoin and carbamazepine, which displace T4 from binding proteins and also induce hepatic metabolism.
Because total T4 is so affected by binding protein changes, free T4 is preferred in most clinical situations. Isolated total T4 results should always be interpreted alongside TSH and free T4 before clinical decisions are made.

Free T4 (Free Thyroxine)
Free T4 is the unbound, biologically active fraction of thyroxine and is the preferred direct measure of thyroid hormone status. Unlike total T4, it is not affected by changes in binding protein concentrations, making it more reliable in pregnancy, liver disease, nephrotic syndrome, and in patients on drugs that alter binding. It is measured alongside TSH and sometimes free T3 to fully assess thyroid status.
Normal free T4 in adults is approximately 9–25 pmol/L (0.7–1.9 ng/dL) using standard immunoassays, though reference ranges vary between laboratories and methods. In the first trimester of pregnancy, free T4 should be interpreted using trimester-specific reference ranges, as values may fall slightly in mid to late pregnancy.
Elevated free T4, particularly with a suppressed TSH, confirms primary hyperthyroidism. Causes include Graves' disease, toxic multinodular goitre, toxic adenoma, subacute thyroiditis (in the initial thyrotoxic phase), post-partum thyroiditis, iodine excess, and thyroid hormone ingestion. Elevated free T4 with a normal or elevated TSH suggests a TSH-secreting pituitary adenoma or thyroid hormone resistance syndrome.
Low free T4 with elevated TSH confirms primary hypothyroidism. Low free T4 with low or normal TSH suggests central (secondary) hypothyroidism due to pituitary or hypothalamic dysfunction, and warrants pituitary imaging and assessment of other axes. During severe non-thyroidal illness, free T4 may also fall transiently without representing true hypothyroidism.
Free T4 markedly above the upper limit of normal (e.g., >50 pmol/L) in a symptomatic patient suggests severe hyperthyroidism or evolving thyroid storm. In a patient with altered consciousness, fever above 40°C, tachyarrhythmia, and markedly elevated free T4, thyroid storm must be immediately suspected and treated as a life-threatening endocrine emergency.

LIPID PANEL
Total Cholesterol
Total cholesterol represents the sum of cholesterol carried in all lipoprotein fractions — LDL, HDL, VLDL, and IDL. Cholesterol is essential for cell membrane integrity, steroid hormone synthesis, bile acid production, and vitamin D metabolism. It is both synthesized endogenously (primarily in the liver) and absorbed from dietary sources. Total cholesterol is a broad cardiovascular risk marker but must be interpreted alongside its component fractions for meaningful clinical use.
Desirable total cholesterol in adults is below 5.0 mmol/L (200 mg/dL). Borderline high is 5.0–6.2 mmol/L (200–239 mg/dL), and high is above 6.2 mmol/L (240 mg/dL). Children and adolescents have lower reference ranges, with desirable levels below 4.4 mmol/L (170 mg/dL). Values naturally increase with age and are higher in post-menopausal women compared to pre-menopausal women of the same age.
Elevated total cholesterol (hypercholesterolaemia) is associated with increased risk of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, coronary artery disease, and stroke. Causes include familial hypercholesterolaemia (FH), hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, obstructive liver disease, poorly controlled diabetes, and high saturated fat diet. Familial hypercholesterolaemia, particularly the homozygous form, can produce total cholesterol above 13–15 mmol/L and causes premature cardiovascular disease in childhood.
Very low total cholesterol (below 2.5 mmol/L) is uncommon and may be associated with malnutrition, hyperthyroidism, severe liver disease, malabsorption syndromes, and rarely familial hypobetalipoproteinaemia. Some studies associate very low cholesterol with increased risk of haemorrhagic stroke, depression, and cancer — though causality is unclear.
Total cholesterol alone is rarely the basis for urgent intervention. However, in the context of an acute cardiovascular event, a lipid panel should be drawn within 24 hours as levels fall after an MI. Extremely high levels (above 13 mmol/L) with xanthomas and family history of early cardiovascular death suggest homozygous FH requiring urgent specialist referral and consideration of apheresis.

HDL (High-Density Lipoprotein)
HDL is a lipoprotein particle that participates in reverse cholesterol transport, carrying cholesterol from peripheral tissues back to the liver for excretion or recycling. It has antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antithrombotic properties that contribute to its cardiovascular protective effects. HDL is synthesized in the liver and intestine and its levels are strongly influenced by genetics, lifestyle, and hormonal status.
Desirable HDL in adults is above 1.0 mmol/L (40 mg/dL) in men and above 1.2 mmol/L (45 mg/dL) in women. Optimal HDL is above 1.5 mmol/L (60 mg/dL), which is considered cardioprotective. Women generally have higher HDL than men due to the effects of oestrogen on lipoprotein metabolism.
High HDL above 2.0 mmol/L is generally considered protective and is associated with reduced cardiovascular risk. However, very high HDL (above 3.0 mmol/L) may paradoxically indicate dysfunctional HDL in some genetic conditions (such as CETP deficiency or LCAT mutations) and may not confer additional protection or may even be associated with increased risk in some studies. Alcohol consumption, moderate aerobic exercise, and oestrogen therapy all raise HDL.
Low HDL (below 1.0 mmol/L in men, below 1.2 mmol/L in women) is an independent cardiovascular risk factor. It is associated with insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, obesity, physical inactivity, cigarette smoking, hypertriglyceridaemia, and anabolic steroid use. Low HDL combined with high triglycerides and central obesity is the hallmark dyslipidaemia of metabolic syndrome.
HDL levels do not typically require urgent action in isolation. However, persistently low HDL combined with other cardiovascular risk factors demands aggressive risk factor modification and consideration of statin therapy, even when LDL appears borderline.

LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein)
LDL is the primary carrier of cholesterol to peripheral tissues and is the main atherogenic lipoprotein. It delivers cholesterol to cells via LDL receptors, but excess circulating LDL, particularly oxidised LDL, accumulates in arterial walls, initiating and propagating atherosclerotic plaques. LDL is typically calculated using the Friedewald equation (Total cholesterol − HDL − Triglycerides/5 in mg/dL) rather than directly measured in routine practice, though direct measurement is preferred when triglycerides are elevated.
Optimal LDL is below 2.6 mmol/L (100 mg/dL) for average-risk adults, below 1.8 mmol/L (70 mg/dL) for those with established cardiovascular disease or diabetes, and below 1.4 mmol/L (55 mg/dL) for very high-risk patients including those with recent ACS. Near-optimal is 2.6–3.3 mmol/L. High is above 4.1 mmol/L (160 mg/dL).
Elevated LDL is the primary driver of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease. It is caused by familial hypercholesterolaemia (heterozygous FH: typically 5.0–9.0 mmol/L; homozygous FH: above 13 mmol/L), hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, obstructive jaundice, and dietary saturated and trans fat excess. Every 1 mmol/L reduction in LDL through statin therapy reduces cardiovascular events by approximately 20–25%.
Low LDL, particularly below 1.0 mmol/L, can occur with aggressive lipid-lowering therapy (high-intensity statins plus ezetimibe plus PCSK9 inhibitors), familial hypobetalipoproteinaemia, severe liver disease, or malnutrition. Current evidence does not identify a threshold below which LDL becomes harmful, and trials of very low LDL have shown continued cardiovascular benefit without excess safety signals.
In the context of an acute myocardial infarction, LDL should be drawn immediately on admission and high-intensity statin therapy initiated regardless of the level. LDL above 5.0 mmol/L warrants investigation for familial hypercholesterolaemia and cascade testing of first-degree relatives.

Triglycerides
Triglycerides are the main storage form of dietary fat, composed of glycerol esterified with three fatty acids. They are packaged into chylomicrons after intestinal absorption and into VLDL in the liver. Elevated triglycerides contribute to cardiovascular risk and, at very high levels, to acute pancreatitis. Fasting is required for accurate measurement, as postprandial levels rise significantly after meals.
Normal fasting triglycerides are below 1.7 mmol/L (150 mg/dL). Borderline high is 1.7–2.2 mmol/L, high is 2.3–5.6 mmol/L, and very high (associated with pancreatitis risk) is above 5.6 mmol/L (500 mg/dL). Reference ranges are the same for men and women, though pregnancy can cause significant triglyceride elevation.
Elevated triglycerides (hypertriglyceridaemia) are caused by uncontrolled type 2 diabetes, obesity, heavy alcohol consumption, hypothyroidism, chronic kidney disease, high carbohydrate diet, genetic familial hypertriglyceridaemia, familial combined hyperlipidaemia, and medications including beta-blockers, thiazides, corticosteroids, retinoids, and antipsychotics. Moderate hypertriglyceridaemia is strongly associated with metabolic syndrome and insulin resistance.
Low fasting triglycerides are not clinically concerning. Very low levels may reflect malnutrition, hyperthyroidism, fat malabsorption, or use of triglyceride-lowering medications, but have no direct pathological significance.
Triglycerides above 10 mmol/L (885 mg/dL) carry significant risk of acute pancreatitis and require urgent treatment, including dietary fat restriction, alcohol cessation, optimisation of glycaemic control, and consideration of fibrate therapy or omega-3 fatty acids. Triglycerides above 20 mmol/L demand urgent specialist review and may require plasmapheresis if pancreatitis is present or imminent.

VLDL (Very Low-Density Lipoprotein)
VLDL is a triglyceride-rich lipoprotein assembled in the liver for transporting endogenously synthesized triglycerides to peripheral adipose and muscle tissue. As VLDL delivers triglycerides, it is progressively converted to IDL and ultimately to LDL. VLDL is typically estimated rather than directly measured in routine lipid panels, calculated as triglycerides divided by 5 (in mg/dL) or triglycerides divided by 2.2 (in mmol/L) using the Friedewald framework.
Normal calculated VLDL cholesterol is approximately 0.1–0.9 mmol/L (2–38 mg/dL). Like triglycerides, VLDL levels are sensitive to diet, fasting status, and metabolic state. Values are not meaningfully interpretable in non-fasting samples.
Elevated VLDL reflects excess hepatic triglyceride synthesis and secretion, occurring in the same conditions that cause hypertriglyceridaemia: type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, obesity, excessive alcohol use, chronic kidney disease, and familial hypertriglyceridaemia. Elevated VLDL is also a feature of familial combined hyperlipidaemia, where it coexists with elevated LDL. High VLDL contributes to the atherogenic dyslipidaemia profile of small dense LDL particles, which are more susceptible to oxidation and arterial wall infiltration.
Low VLDL is not clinically significant and typically reflects low triglyceride production from dietary fat restriction, lean body composition, or effective lipid-lowering therapy. It requires no specific action.
VLDL is not independently monitored for urgent clinical decisions — it is assessed as part of the total lipid picture. However, when it is dramatically elevated as part of severe hypertriglyceridaemia, management of the triglyceride level takes precedence.

DIABETES MARKERS
Fasting Glucose
Fasting plasma glucose measures blood glucose concentration after a minimum of 8 hours without caloric intake. Glucose is the primary metabolic fuel for the brain and body, regulated by insulin (which promotes cellular uptake and storage) and glucagon (which promotes hepatic glucose release). Fasting glucose reflects basal hepatic glucose production and insulin sensitivity, and is the foundational test for diagnosing and monitoring diabetes and prediabetes.
Normal fasting glucose is 3.9–5.5 mmol/L (70–99 mg/dL). Impaired fasting glucose (prediabetes) is 5.6–6.9 mmol/L (100–125 mg/dL). Diabetes is diagnosed at fasting glucose of 7.0 mmol/L (126 mg/dL) or above on two separate occasions, or once in the presence of classic symptoms. Reference ranges are the same across adult age groups and sexes, though older adults may have higher physiological postprandial levels.
Elevated fasting glucose beyond 7.0 mmol/L indicates type 1 or type 2 diabetes mellitus, maturity-onset diabetes of the young (MODY), pancreatic disease (pancreatitis, pancreatic cancer, cystic fibrosis), or secondary diabetes from Cushing's syndrome, acromegaly, or glucocorticoid therapy. Stress hyperglycaemia occurs during acute illness and may not represent true diabetes. Haemochromatosis can also cause diabetes through pancreatic iron deposition.
Low fasting glucose (hypoglycaemia) is below 3.9 mmol/L (70 mg/dL), with symptoms typically at below 3.0 mmol/L. Causes include insulin or sulphonylurea excess (iatrogenic), insulinoma, Addison's disease, pituitary insufficiency, severe liver failure, alcohol excess (which inhibits gluconeogenesis), prolonged fasting, and sepsis in critically ill patients.
Fasting glucose above 25–30 mmol/L with symptoms of diabetic ketoacidosis (vomiting, abdominal pain, Kussmaul breathing, ketones) or hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state (profound dehydration, altered consciousness, glucose often above 33 mmol/L without significant ketosis) requires immediate emergency management. Symptomatic hypoglycaemia below 2.5 mmol/L is a medical emergency requiring immediate glucose administration.

HbA1c (Glycated Haemoglobin)
HbA1c measures the percentage of haemoglobin that has been glycated through non-enzymatic attachment of glucose to the beta-chain of haemoglobin A. Because red blood cells live approximately 90–120 days, HbA1c reflects average blood glucose exposure over the preceding 2–3 months. It is the gold standard for long-term glycaemic monitoring in diabetes and is used diagnostically for diabetes and prediabetes.
Normal HbA1c is below 42 mmol/mol (6.0%) in adults. Prediabetes is defined as 42–47 mmol/mol (6.0–6.4%). Diabetes is diagnosed at 48 mmol/mol (6.5%) or above on two occasions, or once with symptoms. Target HbA1c for most people with diabetes is below 53 mmol/mol (7.0%), though this is individualised — tighter control (48 mmol/mol) for younger patients and less stringent targets (58–64 mmol/mol) for frail elderly patients. Reference ranges do not significantly differ by sex.
Elevated HbA1c indicates sustained hyperglycaemia and poor glycaemic control. Values above 64 mmol/mol (8%) signal significant under-treatment and are associated with substantially increased risk of microvascular complications (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy) and macrovascular disease. HbA1c can be falsely elevated by iron deficiency anaemia (which extends red cell lifespan) and by certain haemoglobin variants.
Low HbA1c is associated with haemolytic anaemia, acute blood loss, haemoglobinopathies (HbS, HbC), recent blood transfusion, pregnancy (which increases red cell turnover), and chronic renal failure — all of which reduce red cell lifespan and therefore reduce glycation time. In these conditions, HbA1c underestimates true glycaemic exposure and fructosamine or continuous glucose monitoring should be used instead.
HbA1c alone is rarely an emergency indicator, but a result of 75 mmol/mol (9%) or higher in a newly presenting patient warrants urgent clinical review and initiation or intensification of therapy, particularly if accompanied by weight loss, polyuria, or other symptoms of severe hyperglycaemia.

Insulin
Insulin is a peptide hormone produced by pancreatic beta cells in response to rising blood glucose, amino acids, and gut hormones. It promotes glucose uptake into muscle and adipose tissue, suppresses hepatic glucose output, stimulates protein and fat synthesis, and inhibits lipolysis and gluconeogenesis. Measuring fasting serum insulin, often alongside fasting glucose, enables calculation of insulin resistance indices such as HOMA-IR. Insulin measurement is not routinely used to diagnose diabetes.
Normal fasting serum insulin in healthy adults is approximately 18–173 pmol/L (2.6–25 µIU/mL), with significant variation by laboratory and assay. HOMA-IR (fasting insulin × fasting glucose / 22.5 using SI units) is typically below 2.0 in insulin-sensitive individuals, with values above 2.5–3.0 suggesting insulin resistance. Reference ranges in children vary with Tanner stage and BMI.
Elevated fasting insulin indicates insulin resistance — the hallmark of type 2 diabetes, prediabetes, and metabolic syndrome. It is also elevated in polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), obesity, Cushing's syndrome, acromegaly, lipodystrophy, and in patients taking corticosteroids or antipsychotics. Inappropriately elevated insulin in the presence of hypoglycaemia suggests endogenous hyperinsulinism from an insulinoma, beta cell hyperplasia, or surreptitious insulin or sulphonylurea ingestion. Measuring C-peptide alongside insulin can differentiate exogenous insulin administration (low C-peptide) from endogenous overproduction (high C-peptide).
Low fasting insulin in the context of hyperglycaemia suggests type 1 diabetes or advanced type 2 diabetes with beta cell failure, both of which require insulin therapy. Low insulin with normal glucose simply reflects insulin sensitivity and is physiologically desirable.
Elevated insulin in the setting of confirmed hypoglycaemia (below 2.2 mmol/L) is clinically urgent — an inappropriately normal or high insulin level when glucose is critically low is diagnostic of hyperinsulinism and requires immediate investigation for insulinoma or surreptitious drug use, alongside acute glucose management.

VITAMINS AND MINERALS
Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin)
Vitamin B12 is a water-soluble vitamin essential for DNA synthesis, red blood cell formation, neurological function, and myelin synthesis. It is found exclusively in animal products and requires intrinsic factor (secreted by gastric parietal cells) for absorption in the terminal ileum. Body stores are substantial (2–5 mg), primarily in the liver, sufficient for 3–5 years of deficiency before symptoms emerge. B12 functions as a cofactor in the conversion of homocysteine to methionine and in the isomerization of methylmalonyl-CoA to succinyl-CoA.
Normal serum B12 is approximately 150–700 pmol/L (200–950 pg/mL) in adults. Values below 150 pmol/L suggest deficiency, while 150–220 pmol/L represents a grey zone where deficiency is possible and functional markers (homocysteine, methylmalonic acid) should be assessed. Reference ranges do not differ significantly by age in adults, though B12 absorption tends to decline with age.
Elevated B12 occurs with supplementation, hepatic disease (liver stores release B12 on hepatocyte destruction), myeloproliferative disorders (particularly CML and polycythaemia vera), and solid organ malignancies. Unexplained persistently elevated B12 in an unsupplemented patient warrants investigation for underlying malignancy or liver disease.
Low B12 causes megaloblastic anaemia (macrocytic anaemia with hypersegmented neutrophils), glossitis, and importantly, subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord — a neurological syndrome affecting the dorsal and lateral columns, causing peripheral neuropathy, ataxia, and proprioceptive loss. Causes include pernicious anaemia (autoimmune destruction of intrinsic factor), strict veganism, gastrectomy, terminal ileal resection or disease, metformin use (which reduces B12 absorption), and proton pump inhibitor use.
Severely low B12 (below 74 pmol/L / 100 pg/mL) with neurological symptoms requires urgent intramuscular B12 replacement to prevent irreversible spinal cord damage. Neurological manifestations of B12 deficiency can progress without significant anaemia and should not be delayed pending further investigation.

Vitamin D (25-Hydroxyvitamin D)
25-hydroxyvitamin D (25-OHD) is the major circulating form of vitamin D and the standard measurement for assessing vitamin D status. It is converted from dietary vitamin D and skin-synthesized cholecalciferol in the liver, then further hydroxylated in the kidney to the active form, 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol). Vitamin D regulates calcium and phosphate absorption from the gut, promotes bone mineralisation, modulates immune function, and influences cell differentiation.
Sufficient vitamin D is generally defined as 25-OHD above 50 nmol/L (20 ng/mL). Insufficiency is 30–50 nmol/L (12–20 ng/mL) and deficiency below 30 nmol/L (12 ng/mL). Optimal levels for bone health and other functions are debated, with some guidelines suggesting above 75 nmol/L (30 ng/mL). Reference ranges are the same across adult sexes, though darker skin, obesity, limited sun exposure, and aging all reduce vitamin D synthesis. Neonates require supplementation as breast milk is low in vitamin D.
Elevated vitamin D (above 250 nmol/L / 100 ng/mL) is almost always iatrogenic from supplement over-dosing. Toxicity causes hypercalcaemia with symptoms of nausea, vomiting, polyuria, polydipsia, confusion, and renal calcification. Granulomatous diseases (sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, fungal infections) and some lymphomas can also cause elevated 25-OHD or 1,25-OHD through unregulated extra-renal hydroxylation.
Low vitamin D causes rickets in children (bowed legs, widened growth plates, delayed fontanelle closure) and osteomalacia in adults (bone pain, muscle weakness, stress fractures). Severe deficiency is associated with secondary hyperparathyroidism, hypocalcaemia, and proximal myopathy. Risk factors include limited sun exposure, darker skin, obesity, malabsorption, chronic liver or kidney disease, and anticonvulsant use.
Vitamin D below 12.5 nmol/L (5 ng/mL) with symptomatic hypocalcaemia, muscle weakness, or confirmed osteomalacia is clinically urgent and warrants immediate high-dose replacement, monitoring of calcium, and investigation for underlying malabsorption.

Iron (Serum Iron)
Serum iron measures the concentration of iron bound to transferrin in the circulation. It fluctuates significantly throughout the day (diurnal variation of up to 30%), with levels highest in the morning and lower in the evening. It should always be interpreted alongside transferrin (or TIBC) and ferritin to characterise iron status accurately. Iron is essential for haemoglobin synthesis, myoglobin, cytochrome enzymes, and numerous other enzymatic functions.
Normal serum iron in adults is approximately 10–30 µmol/L (60–170 µg/dL) in men and 8–25 µmol/L (50–140 µg/dL) in women. Due to significant diurnal variation and sensitivity to recent dietary iron intake, serum iron has limited diagnostic precision when used alone.
Elevated serum iron occurs in iron overload states — hereditary haemochromatosis (HFE gene mutations), transfusional iron overload in haematological diseases (thalassaemia, sickle cell disease, myelodysplastic syndrome), excessive oral iron supplementation, and haemolytic anaemia. It also rises in liver disease (released from damaged hepatocytes) and in aplastic anaemia (where iron accumulates due to reduced erythropoiesis). Elevated iron with high transferrin saturation (above 45%) warrants genetic testing for haemochromatosis.
Low serum iron occurs in iron deficiency anaemia (from blood loss, inadequate intake, or malabsorption), anaemia of chronic disease (where iron is sequestered in stores but unavailable for erythropoiesis), pregnancy, and in the acute phase response (iron is a negative acute-phase reactant).
Extremely high serum iron (above 90 µmol/L / 500 µg/dL) in the context of acute iron poisoning is a medical emergency, particularly in children. Symptoms include vomiting, haemorrhagic gastroenteritis, shock, and hepatic failure, requiring immediate deferoxamine chelation therapy.

Ferritin
Ferritin is the primary intracellular iron storage protein, found in all cells but released into serum in proportion to total body iron stores. It is the most reliable single test for diagnosing iron deficiency, as it falls before serum iron or haemoglobin changes. However, ferritin is also an acute phase reactant, meaning it rises substantially during inflammation, infection, malignancy, and liver disease, potentially masking iron deficiency in these conditions.
Normal serum ferritin is approximately 30–300 µg/L in adult men and 13–150 µg/L in pre-menopausal women. Post-menopausal women approach male values. Ferritin below 15 µg/L is diagnostic of iron deficiency regardless of haemoglobin. In inflammatory states, ferritin may be above 100 µg/L even in the presence of true iron deficiency.
Elevated ferritin indicates iron overload (hereditary haemochromatosis, transfusional overload), liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis, alcoholic liver disease), haematological malignancies (leukaemia, lymphoma), other malignancies, systemic inflammatory conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, adult-onset Still's disease), and infection. Extremely high ferritin (above 10,000 µg/L) is characteristic of haemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH), macrophage activation syndrome, and adult-onset Still's disease — rare but life-threatening conditions.
Low ferritin is the earliest and most sensitive indicator of iron deficiency. Symptoms of iron deficiency include fatigue, pallor, pica, koilonychia, glossitis, and eventually iron-deficiency anaemia. Low ferritin with microcytic anaemia and low reticulocyte count strongly supports iron-deficiency anaemia.
Ferritin above 10,000 µg/L should prompt urgent investigation for HLH, which is a hyperinflammatory syndrome with high mortality requiring immediate haematology review and consideration of immunosuppression. Ferritin above 1,000 µg/L in an acutely ill patient should prompt evaluation for haemochromatosis, malignancy, and severe inflammatory disease.

Folate (Folic Acid)
Folate is a water-soluble B vitamin (B9) that is essential for DNA synthesis and repair, particularly for the methylation of deoxyuridine to thymidine. It also works alongside B12 in the one-carbon metabolism cycle, converting homocysteine to methionine. Body stores are much smaller than B12 (3–10 mg), sufficient for only 3–4 months, making deficiency common during periods of increased demand or poor intake. Neural tube defects in the developing embryo are directly caused by folate deficiency in early pregnancy.
Normal serum folate is approximately 7–45 nmol/L (3–20 ng/mL). Red cell folate (10–450 nmol/L) better reflects long-term tissue stores, as serum folate reflects recent intake rather than total body stores and can normalise rapidly after a single folate-rich meal. Reference ranges are the same for adult men and women.
Elevated folate has no known clinical harm and is common in patients taking folic acid supplements. It does not require investigation or treatment.
Low folate causes megaloblastic anaemia identical to B12 deficiency (macrocytic anaemia, hypersegmented neutrophils, glossitis) but without the neurological features of B12 deficiency. Common causes include poor dietary intake (alcoholism, poverty, food insecurity, eating disorders), malabsorption (coeliac disease, Crohn's disease, short bowel syndrome), increased demand (pregnancy, haemolytic anaemia, haemodialysis, psoriasis), and drugs that antagonise folate metabolism (methotrexate, trimethoprim, phenytoin, sulfasalazine). In pregnancy, deficiency during the first four weeks causes neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly).
Folate deficiency in early pregnancy is the primary clinical urgency. All women of childbearing age should receive 400 µg/day supplementation preconceptually and through the first 12 weeks, with 5 mg/day for high-risk women (previous NTD, diabetes, anticonvulsant use, BMI above 30). Active deficiency with megaloblastic anaemia warrants prompt oral or parenteral replacement.

Calcium (Total Serum Calcium)
Calcium is the most abundant mineral in the body, with 99% stored in bone as hydroxyapatite and 1% in the circulation and intracellular fluid. Circulating calcium exists in three fractions: ionised (biologically active, ~50%), protein-bound (mainly to albumin, ~40%), and complexed to anions (~10%). Total serum calcium includes all three fractions and must be corrected for albumin level (corrected calcium = measured calcium + 0.02 × (40 − albumin in g/L)). Calcium regulates neuromuscular excitability, cardiac conduction, coagulation, and hormone secretion, and is tightly regulated by PTH and vitamin D.
Normal total serum calcium is 2.1–2.6 mmol/L (8.5–10.5 mg/dL) in adults. Corrected calcium should always be used when albumin is abnormal. Ionised calcium (the truly relevant fraction) is 1.15–1.35 mmol/L. Neonates have transient hypocalcaemia in the first days of life. Children and adolescents have similar ranges to adults.
Elevated calcium (hypercalcaemia) above 2.6 mmol/L is most commonly caused by primary hyperparathyroidism (usually from a parathyroid adenoma, presenting with mild persistent hypercalcaemia) and malignancy (bone metastases, PTHrP secretion in squamous cell carcinoma, haematological malignancies). Other causes include sarcoidosis and other granulomatous diseases, vitamin D toxicity, thiazide diuretics, immobilisation, milk-alkali syndrome, and familial hypocalciuric hypercalcaemia. Symptoms of hypercalcaemia are summarised as "bones, stones, groans, and psychic moans" — bone pain, renal calculi, abdominal pain, and neuropsychiatric symptoms.
Low calcium (hypocalcaemia) causes increased neuromuscular excitability, presenting with perioral tingling, paraesthesia, tetany, Chvostek's sign (facial muscle twitch on tapping the facial nerve), Trousseau's sign (carpopedal spasm on BP cuff inflation), and in severe cases, laryngospasm, bronchospasm, and seizures. Causes include hypoparathyroidism (post-thyroidectomy, autoimmune), vitamin D deficiency, hypomagnesaemia (impairs PTH secretion), acute pancreatitis, renal failure, malabsorption, and hungry bone syndrome post-parathyroidectomy.
Corrected calcium above 3.5 mmol/L (14 mg/dL) is a hypercalcaemic crisis requiring emergency IV fluid resuscitation, bisphosphonates, and investigation for underlying cause. Ionised calcium below 0.8 mmol/L with cardiac arrhythmia, laryngospasm, or seizures requires urgent IV calcium gluconate or chloride.

Magnesium
Magnesium is the second most abundant intracellular cation and a cofactor in over 300 enzymatic reactions, including ATP synthesis, protein synthesis, DNA replication, and neuromuscular transmission. It is essential for the proper functioning of sodium-potassium ATPase and calcium channels. Approximately 60% is stored in bone, 20% in muscle, and only 1% circulates in serum — making serum magnesium an insensitive marker of total body stores. Low serum magnesium usually reflects significant deficiency.
Normal serum magnesium is 0.7–1.1 mmol/L (1.7–2.6 mg/dL) in adults. Reference ranges are similar across sexes. Serum magnesium is relatively stable throughout adulthood.
Elevated magnesium (hypermagnesaemia) is almost always iatrogenic, most commonly from excessive magnesium sulphate administration (used in eclampsia management, as a cathartic, or for constipation in patients with renal impairment who cannot excrete it). It also occurs in chronic kidney disease and Addison's disease. Symptoms include nausea, flushing, decreased reflexes, muscle weakness, respiratory depression, and cardiac arrest at very high levels.
Low magnesium (hypomagnesaemia) causes muscle cramps, tremor, personality change, cardiac arrhythmias (particularly torsades de pointes), and refractory hypokalaemia and hypocalcaemia (because magnesium is required for PTH secretion and for potassium channel function). Common causes include chronic diarrhoea, alcohol excess, proton pump inhibitor use (which impairs intestinal magnesium absorption), aminoglycoside and cisplatin nephrotoxicity, loop diuretics, poorly controlled diabetes, and malnutrition.
Magnesium above 2.0 mmol/L requires stopping magnesium infusions and monitoring. Above 3.0 mmol/L with loss of deep tendon reflexes or respiratory depression, calcium gluconate should be given as an antagonist and renal replacement therapy considered. Magnesium below 0.5 mmol/L with cardiac arrhythmia requires urgent IV magnesium sulphate infusion.

CARDIAC MARKERS
Troponin (Troponin I and Troponin T)
Troponin is a regulatory protein complex (consisting of troponin I, T, and C) found in cardiac and skeletal muscle that controls calcium-mediated actin-myosin interaction. Cardiac-specific isoforms — cardiac troponin I (cTnI) and cardiac troponin T (cTnT) — are released into the bloodstream when cardiomyocytes are injured or necrotic. High-sensitivity troponin assays (hs-cTn) can detect minute quantities and have revolutionised the early diagnosis and rule-out of myocardial infarction.
Normal hs-cTnI and hs-cTnT levels are defined as below the 99th percentile of a healthy reference population, which varies by assay and sex. For most current assays, the 99th percentile is approximately 14–26 ng/L for hs-cTnI (women have lower thresholds than men) and around 14 ng/L for hs-cTnT. Values at or below this threshold effectively rule out myocardial infarction when clinical probability is low.
Elevated troponin indicates myocardial injury, which may be due to type 1 MI (plaque rupture with thrombosis), type 2 MI (supply-demand mismatch in the absence of plaque rupture, from tachyarrhythmia, hypotension, sepsis, or severe anaemia), myocarditis, pulmonary embolism (right heart strain), heart failure (chronic elevation), hypertensive emergency, Takotsubo cardiomyopathy, cardiac contusion, renal failure (reduced clearance), or post-ablation. A rising and/or falling pattern (delta troponin) on serial measurements distinguishes acute MI from chronic stable elevation due to non-ischaemic causes.
Low or undetectable troponin effectively rules out significant myocardial necrosis when measured using a high-sensitivity assay at 0 and 1–3 hours. There is no clinical significance to values below the 99th percentile threshold.
Any troponin above the 99th percentile with a rising or falling pattern, in the context of chest pain, dyspnoea, or ECG changes, should be treated as probable acute coronary syndrome until proven otherwise and managed as a medical emergency with immediate cardiology involvement.

CK-MB (Creatine Kinase Myocardial Band)
CK-MB is a dimeric isoenzyme of creatine kinase (CK) found predominantly in cardiac muscle, comprising approximately 20–25% of total CK in the heart. Unlike total CK, which is predominantly from skeletal muscle, CK-MB is relatively cardiac-specific (though small amounts exist in skeletal muscle, especially after intense exercise or in muscle disease). It rises and falls faster than total CK after myocardial injury, making it useful for timing infarction and detecting reinfarction. It has largely been superseded by high-sensitivity troponin for diagnosis but remains useful in specific contexts.
Normal CK-MB is less than 5–10 ng/mL (or below 5% of total CK in mass assays). Activity-based assays express it in U/L, with normal values typically below 25 U/L. Values do not differ significantly by sex, though they may be mildly higher in athletes due to skeletal muscle contribution.
Elevated CK-MB indicates myocardial injury, most specifically in the context of STEMI or NSTEMI. It rises approximately 3–6 hours after infarction, peaks at 12–24 hours, and returns to normal within 36–48 hours. This rapid return to baseline makes it useful for diagnosing reinfarction (a second rise after initial normalisation) and for calculating infarct size. CK-MB can also be elevated in myocarditis, cardiac surgery, electrical cardioversion, and rarely in severe skeletal muscle disease with high total CK.
Normal or low CK-MB in isolation has no clinical significance. When troponin is elevated but CK-MB is disproportionately low, this pattern may suggest chronic myocardial injury rather than acute necrosis.
In the context of chest pain with ECG changes, markedly elevated CK-MB (e.g., above 50–100 ng/mL) alongside elevated troponin is consistent with large myocardial infarction and requires immediate percutaneous coronary intervention or thrombolysis depending on available facilities. CK-MB monitoring over 72 hours is used to assess reperfusion after thrombolysis — an early, sharp peak suggests successful reperfusion.

BNP / NT-proBNP (B-type Natriuretic Peptide)
BNP is a natriuretic peptide hormone secreted primarily by ventricular cardiomyocytes in response to increased wall stress from volume overload or pressure overload. It promotes natriuresis, diuresis, vasodilation, and inhibits the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system. NT-proBNP is the inactive N-terminal fragment cleaved during BNP synthesis — it has a longer half-life (60–120 minutes vs 20 minutes for BNP) and accumulates to higher levels. Both are used to diagnose and stratify severity of heart failure and to monitor treatment response.
Normal BNP is below 100 pg/mL (29 pmol/L). Values below 100 pg/mL effectively rule out heart failure as a cause of dyspnoea. Normal NT-proBNP varies by age: below 125 pg/mL for patients under 75, and below 450 pg/mL for those over 75. Both markers are significantly higher in renal failure (reduced clearance) and lower in obesity (adipose tissue reduces BNP expression). Values are slightly higher in women.
Elevated BNP or NT-proBNP strongly suggests heart failure and correlates with its severity. NYHA class and BNP rise together. Other causes include acute pulmonary embolism (right ventricular strain), pulmonary hypertension, acute coronary syndrome, cor pulmonale, myocarditis, sepsis (direct myocardial depression), and renal failure. In the emergency setting, a markedly elevated BNP in a patient with dyspnoea distinguishes cardiac from pulmonary causes and guides diuretic therapy.
Very low BNP in a dyspnoeic patient effectively excludes heart failure and redirects investigation toward pulmonary, infective, or metabolic causes. Notably, obesity causes falsely low BNP; clinical judgement is needed if heart failure is strongly suspected in an obese patient with low BNP.
BNP above 400 pg/mL or NT-proBNP above 2000 pg/mL in an acutely dyspnoeic patient is indicative of decompensated heart failure requiring urgent IV diuresis, oxygen, and monitoring. NT-proBNP above 5000 pg/mL is associated with high short-term mortality and may warrant ICU-level care.

INFLAMMATORY MARKERS
CRP (C-Reactive Protein)
CRP is an acute phase protein synthesized by the liver in response to inflammatory cytokines, primarily IL-6. It rises rapidly (within 4–6 hours) in response to tissue injury, infection, or inflammation, making it an excellent sensitive but non-specific marker of systemic inflammation. High-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) can detect very low levels and is used in cardiovascular risk stratification. Standard CRP is used to monitor acute infection and inflammatory conditions.
Normal standard CRP is below 10 mg/L in healthy adults. Hs-CRP for cardiovascular risk: below 1 mg/L is low risk, 1–3 mg/L is intermediate risk, and above 3 mg/L is elevated risk. Reference ranges are similar across sexes and age groups, though CRP rises modestly with obesity and smoking even in the absence of acute illness.
Elevated CRP occurs in bacterial infection (typically causing greater elevations than viral infection), autoimmune disease flares (rheumatoid arthritis, vasculitis, SLE — though CRP can be paradoxically normal in SLE flares), inflammatory bowel disease, malignancy, myocardial infarction, surgical trauma, burns, and pancreatitis. Very high CRP (above 100 mg/L) is strongly associated with bacterial infection, particularly pneumonia, sepsis, and bacterial peritonitis. CRP above 200 mg/L suggests severe bacterial infection or tissue necrosis.
Low or undetectable CRP effectively argues against active significant bacterial infection or severe inflammation in the appropriate clinical context, making it useful for antibiotic stewardship and to support watchful waiting in mild illness.
CRP above 200–300 mg/L in a febrile patient suggests severe bacterial sepsis or extensive tissue necrosis and should prompt immediate blood cultures, source control, and broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy. However, CRP should always be interpreted alongside clinical findings and other investigations — a high CRP alone does not mandate antibiotics, and a normal CRP does not exclude serious infection.

ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate)
ESR measures the rate at which red blood cells settle in a vertical tube of anticoagulated blood over one hour. Elevated fibrinogen, globulins, and other acute-phase proteins cause red cells to aggregate (rouleaux formation) and sediment more rapidly. ESR is sensitive but highly non-specific and slow to respond to changes in clinical status compared to CRP. It is most useful for monitoring chronic inflammatory conditions such as temporal arteritis, polymyalgia rheumatica, and multiple myeloma.
Normal ESR in men is approximately 0–15 mm/hour below age 50 and 0–20 mm/hour above 50. In women, normal is 0–20 mm/hour below 50 and 0–30 mm/hour above 50. A useful approximation is (age / 2) for men and (age + 10) / 2 for women. Pregnancy significantly raises ESR. Anaemia increases ESR by reducing RBC concentration, while polycythaemia reduces it.
Elevated ESR occurs in infection, autoimmune and inflammatory conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, temporal arteritis, polymyalgia rheumatica, inflammatory bowel disease), malignancy (particularly multiple myeloma, lymphoma, solid tumours), renal failure, thyroid disease, and anaemia. An ESR above 100 mm/hour narrows the differential significantly — the most common causes are malignancy, infection (particularly TB, infective endocarditis, osteomyelitis), and autoimmune disease.
Very low ESR is associated with polycythaemia vera, sickle cell disease, hereditary spherocytosis, hypofibrinogenaemia, congestive heart failure, and hyperviscosity syndromes. It rarely has direct clinical implications but can indicate one of these underlying conditions.
An ESR above 100 mm/hour in an elderly patient with new headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, and visual symptoms is a clinical emergency suggesting giant cell arteritis, where delay in high-dose corticosteroid therapy risks permanent blindness. In this specific context, treatment should not wait for biopsy confirmation.

Procalcitonin
Procalcitonin (PCT) is the precursor peptide of calcitonin, produced by thyroid C cells under normal circumstances in negligible amounts. During bacterial infection, however, virtually every tissue in the body can produce PCT in response to bacterial toxins and pro-inflammatory cytokines (particularly IL-6, TNF-alpha, and IL-1). It rises within 4–6 hours of bacterial infection, peaks at 24 hours, and has a half-life of approximately 24 hours. Because it is specifically induced by bacterial endotoxin and suppressed during viral infection, it is more specific for bacterial infection than CRP or ESR.
Normal PCT is below 0.1 µg/L in healthy adults. Values below 0.25 µg/L argue against significant bacterial infection. Values between 0.25 and 0.5 µg/L suggest possible bacterial infection, and above 0.5 µg/L indicate probable bacterial infection warranting antibiotics. Reference ranges are the same across sexes and adult age groups.
Markedly elevated PCT (above 2 µg/L) indicates high likelihood of systemic bacterial infection or sepsis. PCT above 10 µg/L is associated with severe sepsis and bacteraemia. It is used in ICU settings to guide antibiotic initiation and duration — serial declining PCT values support antibiotic de-escalation and earlier cessation, reducing antibiotic overuse. PCT can also be elevated in non-infectious causes including major surgery, severe burns, multiple trauma, and acute pancreatitis.
Low PCT in a febrile patient supports a viral aetiology (influenza, COVID-19, other respiratory viruses) and is used in antibiotic stewardship programs to withhold or discontinue antibiotics in community-acquired lower respiratory tract infections.
PCT above 10 µg/L with clinical signs of sepsis (tachycardia, hypotension, altered consciousness, new organ dysfunction) is a medical emergency. Immediate blood cultures, broad-spectrum antibiotics within one hour, and aggressive IV fluid resuscitation should be initiated without delay, as each hour of delay in sepsis treatment increases mortality significantly.

URINE ANALYSIS
Urine Protein
Normally, the glomerular filtration membrane restricts passage of large proteins such as albumin, and small proteins that are filtered are reabsorbed by proximal tubular cells. As a result, normal urine protein excretion is less than 150 mg/day, below the detection threshold of standard dipstick tests. Proteinuria represents either glomerular damage (increased permeability to proteins), tubular damage (failure to reabsorb filtered proteins), or overflow of a small filtered protein (as in multiple myeloma with Bence Jones protein).
Normal dipstick result is negative or trace (below 0.3 g/L). Significant proteinuria on dipstick is 1+ (approximately 0.3 g/L) or greater. Quantitative assessment uses a spot urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR): below 3 mg/mmol is normal, 3–30 mg/mmol is microalbuminuria (moderately increased), and above 30 mg/mmol is macroalbuminuria (severely increased). In pregnancy, proteinuria above 300 mg/24h or ACR above 30 mg/mmol combined with hypertension defines pre-eclampsia.
Elevated urine protein signals glomerular pathology — diabetic nephropathy (the most common cause of proteinuria globally), IgA nephropathy, focal segmental glomerulosclerosis, membranous nephropathy, minimal change disease, lupus nephritis, and pre-eclampsia. Nephrotic syndrome is defined by proteinuria above 3.5 g/day, hypoalbuminaemia, oedema, and hyperlipidaemia. Tubular proteinuria from AKI, toxic nephropathy, or Fanconi syndrome produces smaller molecular weight proteins not detected by standard albumin dipsticks.
Trace or absent proteinuria in a person without hypertension or diabetes makes significant renal disease less likely, though tubular proteinuria and Bence Jones proteinuria will be missed by standard dipstick.
Proteinuria above 3.5 g/day (nephrotic range) warrants urgent nephrology referral. In pregnancy, new proteinuria combined with hypertension and systemic symptoms (headache, visual disturbance, right upper quadrant pain, thrombocytopenia) constitutes severe pre-eclampsia or evolving HELLP syndrome — a obstetric emergency requiring immediate specialist management and consideration of delivery.

Urine Glucose
Glucose is freely filtered at the glomerulus and completely reabsorbed in the proximal tubule via sodium-glucose co-transporters (SGLT1/2) under normal circumstances. Glucosuria occurs when the blood glucose exceeds the renal threshold for reabsorption (approximately 10 mmol/L or 180 mg/dL in most individuals), overwhelming tubular capacity. Detection of urine glucose on dipstick therefore most commonly signals hyperglycaemia, though proximal tubular dysfunction can cause glucosuria at normal blood glucose levels.
Normal urine glucose is negative (below 0.8 mmol/L or 15 mg/dL). Dipstick tests are specific for glucose via the glucose oxidase reaction and do not detect other reducing sugars such as galactose or fructose, which require Clinitest tablets for detection.
Positive urine glucose most commonly indicates diabetes mellitus or stress-induced hyperglycaemia, where blood glucose exceeds the renal threshold. It should prompt measurement of fasting blood glucose or HbA1c. Less commonly, glucosuria with normal or low blood glucose indicates a low renal threshold — seen in pregnancy (physiological reduction of threshold), Fanconi syndrome (proximal tubular dysfunction from heavy metal toxicity, Wilson's disease, cystinosis, or tenofovir nephrotoxicity), and genetic primary renal glucosuria (SLC5A2 mutations).
Negative urine glucose in a symptomatic patient does not exclude diabetes, as some individuals have an elevated renal threshold (particularly elderly patients) and can be significantly hyperglycaemic without glucosuria.
Strongly positive urine glucose in a newly presenting patient with polyuria, polydipsia, and weight loss warrants same-day blood glucose measurement to exclude diabetic ketoacidosis or hyperosmolar hyperglycaemic state, particularly if ketones are also present.

Urine Ketones
Ketones (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, and acetone) are produced by the liver from fatty acid oxidation when glucose is unavailable or cannot be utilised — during starvation, prolonged fasting, low-carbohydrate diets, alcohol intoxication, or impaired insulin action. Standard urine dipsticks detect acetoacetate (and to a lesser extent acetone) via the nitroprusside reaction but do not detect beta-hydroxybutyrate, which is the predominant ketone in diabetic ketoacidosis. Blood ketone measurement (beta-hydroxybutyrate) is therefore more accurate for DKA assessment.
Normal urine is ketone-negative or shows trace amounts only (below 0.5 mmol/L). Trace ketonuria can be physiological after overnight fasting. Values of 1+ to 4+ on dipstick represent progressively more significant ketonaemia.
Moderate to heavy ketonuria in the setting of hyperglycaemia (blood glucose above 11 mmol/L) strongly suggests diabetic ketoacidosis in type 1 diabetes or (less commonly) type 2 diabetes. In an euglycaemic or hypoglycaemic patient, ketonuria suggests starvation ketosis, prolonged vomiting, alcoholic ketoacidosis, or ketogenic diet. Ketonuria in a pregnant patient warrants careful evaluation as ketones cross the placenta and may affect fetal brain development.
Absent ketonuria in a patient with suspected DKA does not exclude the diagnosis — blood beta-hydroxybutyrate above 3 mmol/L is the definitive marker and should be measured directly.
Ketonuria of 3+ or 4+ on dipstick in a patient with vomiting, abdominal pain, and hyperglycaemia suggests DKA — a medical emergency requiring immediate IV insulin, IV fluids, electrolyte replacement (particularly potassium), and close monitoring. Blood beta-hydroxybutyrate above 3.0 mmol/L confirms DKA, and the goal of treatment is to clear ketones, normalize pH, and replace fluid and electrolyte losses.

Urine Specific Gravity
Specific gravity (SG) measures the density of urine relative to distilled water (density 1.000), reflecting the concentration of solutes including urea, creatinine, electrolytes, and other dissolved particles. It is a proxy for urine osmolality and assesses the kidney's ability to concentrate or dilute urine, which reflects collecting duct responsiveness to ADH (antidiuretic hormone) and overall renal concentrating capacity.
Normal urine specific gravity ranges from 1.003 to 1.030, depending on hydration status. In a well-hydrated person, SG may be as low as 1.001–1.005. In a dehydrated or oliguric person, SG may reach 1.025–1.030. Fixed SG of 1.010 (isosthenuria) indicates inability to concentrate or dilute urine and is seen in chronic kidney disease with tubular dysfunction.
High specific gravity (above 1.025) indicates concentrated urine from dehydration, fluid restriction, syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion (SIADH), heart failure, or hypovolaemia. Contrast agents and large amounts of glucose or protein in the urine can also falsely elevate SG without reflecting solute concentration.
Low specific gravity (below 1.005) indicates dilute urine, appropriate in overhydrated states but pathological in diabetes insipidus (either central, from ADH deficiency, or nephrogenic, from ADH resistance), excessive water intake (psychogenic polydipsia), and renal failure with loss of concentrating ability.
Fixed isosthenuric urine (consistently 1.010 regardless of hydration status) in the context of rising creatinine, oliguria, or anuria indicates severe loss of tubular function and is a marker of established renal failure. In acute kidney injury with oliguria and SG above 1.020, the cause is more likely pre-renal (tubules are intact and concentrating), while SG around 1.010 in AKI suggests intrinsic tubular damage (acute tubular necrosis).

ELECTROLYTES
Sodium
Sodium is the dominant extracellular cation and the primary determinant of plasma osmolality and extracellular fluid volume. It is tightly regulated through thirst, ADH (which controls water reabsorption in the collecting duct), aldosterone (which controls sodium reabsorption in the distal tubule), and atrial natriuretic peptide. Sodium abnormalities almost always reflect disturbances in water balance rather than absolute sodium excess or deficit.
Normal serum sodium is 135–145 mmol/L in adults and children. The reference range is the same across sexes and stable throughout adulthood, though it may be affected by laboratory measurement techniques (pseudohyponatraemia can occur with severe hyperlipidaemia or hyperproteinaemia in older assays).
Elevated sodium (hypernatraemia) above 145 mmol/L always indicates a relative deficit of water, from water loss in excess of sodium (insensible losses, osmotic diuresis, diabetes insipidus, diarrhoea) or inadequate water intake (altered thirst perception in elderly or unconscious patients). It causes cellular dehydration and neurological symptoms proportional to the rate of rise — irritability, confusion, seizures, and coma.
Low sodium (hyponatraemia) below 135 mmol/L is the most common electrolyte disorder in hospitalised patients. It is classified by volume status: hypovolaemic (diarrhoea, vomiting, diuretics, Addison's disease), euvolaemic (SIADH, hypothyroidism, psychogenic polydipsia), or hypervolaemic (heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome). Symptoms depend on the rate of fall — acute hyponatraemia below 125 mmol/L can cause cerebral oedema, headache, confusion, and seizures.
Sodium below 120 mmol/L with neurological symptoms (seizures, coma) is a medical emergency requiring urgent controlled sodium correction (no faster than 8–10 mmol/L per 24 hours using hypertonic saline if severely symptomatic) to avoid both cerebral oedema and osmotic demyelination syndrome. Sodium above 160 mmol/L with neurological deterioration also requires urgent controlled rehydration.

Potassium
Potassium is the primary intracellular cation, with 98% found inside cells and only 2% in extracellular fluid. This gradient, maintained by the Na-K-ATPase pump, is critical for membrane resting potential and action potential generation in cardiac and neuromuscular tissue. Small changes in serum potassium, which reflects only the extracellular pool, can have profound effects on cardiac rhythm. Potassium is regulated primarily by aldosterone (promoting distal tubular excretion) and by acid-base status (acidosis causes potassium to shift extracellularly, alkalosis causes intracellular shift).
Normal serum potassium is 3.5–5.0 mmol/L in adults. The reference range is the same across sexes and age groups. In neonates and infants, slightly higher values (up to 5.5 mmol/L) may be physiological. Haemolysis during blood collection causes pseudohyperkalaemia by releasing intracellular potassium.
Elevated potassium (hyperkalaemia) above 5.0 mmol/L is caused by reduced renal excretion (chronic kidney disease, acute kidney injury, Addison's disease, hypoaldosteronism, potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs), excessive intake, and shift from cells (acidosis, massive haemolysis, rhabdomyolysis, tumour lysis syndrome, beta-blockade, insulin deficiency). Hyperkalaemia causes progressive ECG changes — peaked T waves, widened QRS, sine wave pattern — and can cause fatal ventricular fibrillation.
Low potassium (hypokalaemia) below 3.5 mmol/L is caused by GI losses (diarrhoea, vomiting, laxative abuse), renal losses (loop and thiazide diuretics, hyperaldosteronism, Cushing's syndrome, Bartter and Gitelman syndromes, hypomagnesaemia), and intracellular shift (insulin, beta-agonists, alkalosis). Hypokalaemia causes muscle weakness, cramps, ileus, polyuria, and cardiac arrhythmias including torsades de pointes, particularly with coexistent hypomagnesaemia or QTc prolongation.
Potassium above 6.5 mmol/L or any potassium with associated ECG changes (peaked T waves, PR prolongation, widened QRS) is a cardiac emergency. IV calcium gluconate (to stabilise the myocardium), insulin with dextrose, and salbutamol nebulisation are immediate temporising measures, followed by definitive potassium removal with furosemide, calcium resonium, or dialysis. Potassium below 2.5 mmol/L with arrhythmia requires urgent IV replacement under cardiac monitoring.

Chloride
Chloride is the major extracellular anion and plays a central role in maintaining electrical neutrality, osmotic pressure, and acid-base balance. It follows sodium passively across membranes and contributes to the generation of hydrochloric acid in the stomach. Chloride is central to the anion gap calculation (Na − (Cl + HCO₃)) and to identifying the type of metabolic acidosis — a normal anion gap metabolic acidosis (hyperchloraemic acidosis) indicates chloride accumulating to replace lost bicarbonate.
Normal serum chloride is 98–107 mmol/L in adults. Reference ranges are stable across sexes and age groups. Chloride is typically measured as part of a standard electrolyte panel.
Elevated chloride (hyperchloraemia) occurs in normal anion gap metabolic acidosis — from diarrhoea (where bicarbonate is lost in stool and chloride is retained), renal tubular acidosis, excessive normal saline infusion (which contains 154 mmol/L of both sodium and chloride, causing hyperchloraemic acidosis), and Addison's disease. It is also seen in mild dehydration and in patients with a primary respiratory alkalosis (compensatory bicarbonate loss leads to chloride retention). Hyperchloraemia per se does not cause symptoms but signals an underlying acid-base disturbance.
Low chloride (hypochloraemia) is seen in prolonged vomiting (loss of gastric hydrochloric acid), nasogastric suction, chronic diuretic use (loop and thiazide diuretics promote chloride excretion), hyponatraemia, and metabolic alkalosis (where bicarbonate replaces chloride as the balancing anion). Hypochloraemic metabolic alkalosis from protracted vomiting or diuretic use is a classic pattern requiring chloride-containing fluid replacement.
Chloride levels do not independently trigger urgent management, but extreme hyperchloraemia (above 115 mmol/L) or hypochloraemia (below 90 mmol/L) should prompt acid-base analysis and investigation for the underlying disturbance. Clinical urgency is determined by the associated acid-base disorder and clinical status rather than chloride alone.

Bicarbonate (Serum HCO₃)
Serum bicarbonate is the main extracellular buffer and a key component of the carbonic acid-bicarbonate buffering system (CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻). It is regulated by the kidneys, which reabsorb filtered bicarbonate and generate new bicarbonate through hydrogen ion excretion. Bicarbonate on a venous chemistry panel (total CO₂) is approximately 1–2 mmol/L higher than true measured bicarbonate on arterial blood gas. It is central to interpreting metabolic acid-base disturbances.
Normal serum bicarbonate (total CO₂) is 22–29 mmol/L in adults. Values are similar across sexes. In children, slightly lower values (18–22 mmol/L in infants) may be normal. At altitude, compensatory hyperventilation and renal bicarbonate wasting may cause mildly lower values.
Elevated bicarbonate (above 29 mmol/L) indicates metabolic alkalosis or respiratory compensation for chronic respiratory acidosis. Metabolic alkalosis arises from loss of hydrogen ions (vomiting, nasogastric suction) or gain of bicarbonate (excess antacid, massive blood transfusion with citrate metabolism, milk-alkali syndrome). Chronic hypercapnia in COPD with CO₂ retention causes the kidney to retain bicarbonate as compensation — these patients may have chronic bicarbonate of 30–35 mmol/L without acute alkalosis.
Low bicarbonate (below 22 mmol/L) indicates metabolic acidosis or respiratory compensation for chronic respiratory alkalosis. Metabolic acidosis may be anion-gap (ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis, uraemic acidosis, toxic ingestions including methanol and ethylene glycol) or normal anion gap (diarrhoea, renal tubular acidosis, chloride administration). Symptoms include Kussmaul breathing (deep, rapid breathing as respiratory compensation), nausea, fatigue, and in severe cases, cardiovascular depression.
Bicarbonate below 12 mmol/L indicates severe metabolic acidosis, particularly if pH is below 7.1 on arterial blood gas. This is a medical emergency — causes include DKA, lactic acidosis (sepsis, shock, mesenteric ischaemia), severe AKI, or toxic ingestion. Urgent management depends on the aetiology: insulin and fluids for DKA, source control and resuscitation for septic lactic acidosis, bicarbonate infusion in severe acidaemia with haemodynamic compromise.
